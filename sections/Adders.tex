

\section*{{ADDERS}}

In electronics, an adder is a digital logic circuit that adds integers together.

Half Adder  
An adder is an electrical circuit that adds numbers together.
A half adder needs two binary inputs and two binary outputs to add two bits.
Adding two single-bit binary values, X and Y, gives you a sum bit S and a carryout bit C-out. .

Full Adder

A complete adder is a type of combinational circuit that adds three bits: two critical bits and a carry from the previous phase.
The first two inputs are the bits that need to be added, and the third input is the carry from the last place. It has two outputs and three outputs. 
Adding two single-bit binary values, X and Y, along with a carry input bit, C-in, gives you a sum bit and a carry-out bit, C-out.

  

  The Boolean expressions for the whole adder are \[ s = xyc_i + x\bar{y}\bar{c_i} + \bar{x}y\bar{c_i} + \bar{x}\bar{y}c_i \] and \[ c_{i+1} = xyc_i + xy\bar{c_i} + x\bar{y}c_i + \bar{x}yc_i. \]

These expressions check to see if the right carry and total values are there by looking at all the different ways the three inputs could be combined.
 The full adder is better than the half adder because it can handle carry propagation, which is necessary for adding numbers with more than one bit.
